Perspectives on Gender Sensitization

A Kanpur-based comprehensive study on gender attitudes, awareness, and equality among youth (n=100)

AG
Anshika Gupta

Over the past few decades, there's been a lot of talk worldwide about making things fairer for everyone regardless of gender. While there's been some progress in different areas, unfair treatment based on gender still happens way too often. That's where gender sensitization comes in.

Gender sensitization is all about helping people understand gender better. It's about learning about the different roles and identities people have, and making sure everyone gets treated fairly. This means challenging unfair ideas and behaviours that keep inequality going.

Why is gender sensitization important? It can change how individuals, communities, and organizations think and act. By encouraging people to really think about things and understand others' experiences, gender sensitization can help break down old prejudices and inequalities. And it's not just about men and women — it also looks at other gender identities like transgender and non-binary folks. Including everyone's experiences is crucial for making sure no one gets left behind.

Lots of groups and organizations are trying to promote gender sensitization through educational programs, policies, and workplace or community initiatives. But even with all these efforts, there are still big challenges — old ideas about men being superior and women being inferior, along with other barriers, make progress slow.

This research paper deals with the opinions and perspectives in relation to gender sensitivity. It wants to understand what works, what doesn't, and why — by studying different viewpoints of men and women, seeking to present a clearer picture of how sensitized our youth is, and offering better advice on how to make things fairer for everyone.

Chapter 1Introduction to the Study

Terminologies and Concepts

Sex vs. Gender

Sex refers to the biological differences between males and females, such as reproductive organs, chromosomes, and hormones. People are usually assigned a sex at birth based on these physical traits. Intersex individuals are born with physical traits that don't fit typical definitions of male or female.

Gender, unlike sex, refers to the roles, behaviours, and identities that society considers appropriate for men and women. It is about how people identify themselves and how they are expected to act based on cultural norms — and it can differ from the sex assigned at birth.

Gender Roles

Gender roles are the behaviours and tasks a society considers appropriate for men and women, learned from a young age and varying between cultures. Traditional gender roles often dictate opportunities and responsibilities deemed appropriate for each gender — over time and across cultures these roles evolve, and in modern contexts are increasingly seen as archaic.

Gender Stereotypes

Gender stereotypes are simplified and often unfair beliefs about how men and women should behave, which can limit people's potential and choices — for example, the idea that "boys don't cry," or that "girls are bad at math."

Patriarchy

Patriarchy is a system where men hold most of the power and influence, and women have less — visible in the fact that most top corporate and political positions are held by men, and that in many families men are seen as heads of household whose opinions carry more weight.

Masculinity

Masculinity refers to traits, behaviours, and roles typically associated with being a man — strength, courage, independence, leadership, assertiveness. These are socially constructed and vary across cultures. Media often promotes a "daring and angry young man" image, and boys may gang up and bully others, normalizing violence.

Gender Equality

Gender equality means men and women have the same opportunities and rights, regardless of gender. India supports the 2030 Sustainable Development Goals; Goal 5 is about gender equality and aims to end all discrimination against women and girls.

Gender-based Violence

Gender-based violence stems from gender inequality and is a major human rights violation worldwide. While both men and women can be victims, most victims are women and girls. It spans three broad categories:

  1. Criminal violence — rape, murder, abduction, kidnapping, dowry deaths
  2. Domestic violence — sexual abuse, wife battering, maltreatment within the home
  3. Social violence — eve-teasing, coercion into female infanticide, forcing a widow to commit sati

Objectives of the Research

  • To explore and analyse the various perspectives on gender sensitization among different demographic groups including age, gender, and educational background.
  • To assess the current level of awareness and understanding of gender sensitization concepts among the youth of Kanpur city.
  • To provide actionable recommendations for the development and improvement of training programs focused on gender sensitization.

Research Questions

  • What are the common perceptions and understandings of gender sensitization among different demographic groups (age, gender, educational background)?
  • What is the current level of awareness and knowledge about gender sensitization among the youth segment of the population?
  • What are the key recommendations for improving gender sensitization efforts in various settings?

Research Hypothesis

  • Youth of Kanpur city between the age group of 18–35 years is fairly gender sensitized.
  • There is a lack of proper training or initiatives for gender sensitizing the youth of Kanpur city in schools and workplaces.

Significance of the Study

This study is significant as it aims to understand the varying attitudes and beliefs about gender roles and equality within a society. By gathering quantitative data, the study can identify prevalent misconceptions and areas of resistance, as well as highlight successful strategies for promoting gender sensitivity. The findings can inform policymakers, educators, and activists with evidence-based insights, and contribute to academic literature by filling gaps in knowledge about public perceptions of gender issues.

Limitations of the Study

  • People may provide answers they believe are socially acceptable rather than telling their true feelings, leading to inaccurate data.
  • The generalization of findings can be limited by small sample size; ensuring a diverse, representative sample is a difficult task.
  • Gender sensitization includes a wide range of attitudes, beliefs, and behaviours that can be challenging to measure accurately using quantitative methods.
  • Quantitative methods often lack the depth of qualitative methods, potentially missing underlying reasons for certain attitudes.
  • This research does not include analysis of the perspectives and sensitization of the LGBTQIA+ communities.

Chapter 2Review of Literature

Aurange (2016) defines gender sensitivity as the ability to identify gender issues — the beginning of gender awareness, which allows people to reduce cultural beliefs and ideas about male and female roles. Deshmukh (2016) describes gender sensitivity as a way to change behaviour by increasing awareness of gender equality.

Narayan Rao and Gingine (2016) defined gender sensitivity as being aware of societal gender assumptions and their effect on behaviours and practices — spotting and challenging stereotypes, ensuring fair treatment, and promoting equality across diverse contexts such as education, workplaces, and media.

Chavan (2016) argues that gender sensitization includes changing conduct and fostering empathy towards both one's own and the other sex, helping people in "examining their personal attitudes and beliefs and questioning the 'realities' they thought they know."

Sebastian (2016) outlines a four-step process for gender sensitization:

  1. Understanding — recognizing and comprehending the perceptions and attitudes individuals hold about gender.
  2. Identifying — pinpointing incorrect or harmful gender perceptions.
  3. Confronting — challenging and questioning established norms and biases, engaging in difficult conversations.
  4. Deciding — making informed decisions about the best solutions to address and rectify gender biases.

Thosare (2016) emphasizes that true transformation regarding gender issues can only occur when youth develop awareness of gender sensitivity, and that comprehensive training is key to achieving it. "Attitude towards gender role is composite and conflicting" (Gul et al., 2012) — people often verbally support the concept of equality, but their behaviour may not align with this stance.

Sharma (2014) explains that gender sensitization involves changing how people behave by making them aware of issues related to gender equality. Kutateladze (2015) explains that gender sensitivity in language involves choosing words that are unbiased and neutral, favouring gender-neutral language.

Reeves and Baden (2000) distinguish between two related concepts:

  • Gender Equity — ensuring men and women achieve similar life outcomes, considering their different needs and interests, by redistributing power and resources to achieve fairness.
  • Gender Equality — giving women the same opportunities as men, including the chance to participate in public activities and decision-making.

Dash et al. (2008) proposed that a person moves through four stages in a gender sensitization process:

Intervention (Sensitization Programme) → Change in perception → Recognition → Accommodation → Action → Impact (Women empowerment)

Thus, gender sensitized persons become instruments of change and bring about further upliftment in society. As social intolerance and violence against certain genders continue to rise, governments have taken steps and laws have changed — yet the gap between genders keeps getting bigger (Thomas, 2016). Gender sensitization is seen as a way to close this gap.

Chapter 3Research Methodology and Design

To investigate perspectives on gender sensitization, a quantitative research methodology was employed using a survey in the form of a questionnaire. The survey, administered through Google Forms, collected responses from 100 participants — a substantial data set for analysis. It was designed to be closed-ended, with multiple-choice questions specifically tailored to measure attitudes, beliefs, and awareness regarding gender issues.

The survey aimed to capture a diverse range of perspectives by combining convenience sampling with stratified sampling. Convenience sampling was used to efficiently distribute the form to a young population, primarily targeting individuals who were easily accessible and willing to participate. Stratified sampling ensured both men and women were adequately represented, enhancing the reliability and validity of the findings.

Data collection involved distributing the survey link online via email, messaging services, and social media platforms to maximize reach and response rate. Once collection was complete, responses were downloaded and analyzed using frequency distribution methods, with results visually represented through bar charts and histograms to make key patterns easier to interpret.

The age range of 18–35 years was chosen to capture the views of a younger demographic — one often at the forefront of social change and likely to hold contemporary perspectives on gender issues.

Chapter 4Findings and Data Analysis

4.1 Demographics

Age (100 responses)

86% of participants were in the age group of 18–25, 10% were 26–35, and 4% fell outside the 18–35 range — forming a young demographic, selected because they are more aware of evolving gender issues and circumstances.

Gender (100 responses)

53% of respondents were male and 47% female — a ratio kept representative of Kanpur city's population to make the research inclusive. The LGBTQIA+ community was not included due to lack of convenient availability of respondents.

Educational Qualification (100 responses)

All respondents had at least a basic school education. 66% were graduates and 24% were post-graduates with a master's degree; none held a doctoral or post-doctoral degree — indicating a basic awareness sufficient to comprehend the survey questions.

4.2 Gender Sensitization Awareness

Have you ever heard of the term "Gender Sensitisation" before? (100 responses)

77% had heard the term previously, while 23% never had — showing that about one-fourth of participants lacked awareness in this area, reflecting some negligence among youth in engaging with gender sensitization programs.

Do you believe that gender is binary (male/female) or non-binary (beyond male/female)? (99 responses)

Only 39.4% correctly identified gender as non-binary and a social construct. The majority (60.6%) were either wrong or unsure — showing that a major share of the population is unaware of the distinction between "sex" and "gender."

Have you ever received education or training on gender-related issues? (93 responses)

Only 39.8% had received training on gender-related issues in some form; 60.2% never had — pointing to a gap in operational machinery for training across government, educational institutions, and workplaces, and possibly a lack of proper implementation of existing programs.

Do you think gender sensitisation should be integrated into school curriculum? (97 responses)

81.4% supported integrating gender sensitization into the school syllabus, 10.3% were unsure, and 8.3% were against it. Very few schools currently include such curricula — respondents were clearly supportive of taking this further.

Do you think gender sensitisation should be included in workplace training programs? (100 responses)

74% supported including gender sensitization in workplace training, 15% were unsure, and 11% were against it. Most workplaces currently lack such training, which contributes to incidents like workplace sexual harassment.

Do you think gender sensitisation is important in fostering healthy relationships? (96 responses)

75% believed gender sensitivity helps maintain healthier relationships, 17.7% were not clearly vocal about it, and 7.3% disagreed — most respondents believed it reduces toxic behaviour and improves mutual understanding.

4.3 Gender Stereotypes

Men are rational and women are emotional. (99 responses)

33.3% agreed, 43.4% disagreed, and 23.2% stayed neutral — reflecting mixed reactions, with more than half of respondents effectively still holding or leaning toward this stereotype.

Women are generally bad drivers. (100 responses)

13% agreed, 66% disagreed, and 21% stayed neutral. Most respondents believe skill depends on training and proficiency rather than gender, defying this stereotype.

It is okay for men to cry. (100 responses)

82% agreed, 13% disagreed, and 5% stayed neutral — a strong rejection of the stereotype that men must always appear strong and are not "allowed" to cry.

Men are strong and women are weak. (100 responses)

16% agreed, 75% disagreed, and 9% stayed neutral — most respondents rejected this preconceived notion outright.

4.4 Gender Roles

Do you think changing diapers, giving kids a bath and feeding the kids are only the mother's responsibilities? (97 responses)

76.3% disagreed that childcare tasks are solely the mother's responsibility, defying predefined societal gender roles; 23.7% agreed.

Do you think a woman's most important role is to look after the home while a man is supposed to be the provider of the family? (98 responses)

65.3% disagreed with this strict role division, while 34.7% agreed with it.

Should the household chores be divided equally if both the man and the woman are working? (98 responses)

93.9% supported equal division of household chores when both partners work; only 6.1% favoured the woman handling both home and work responsibilities, effectively supporting a "double burden."

4.5 Gender Equality

Do you think women and men should receive equal pay for equal work? (98 responses)

90.8% favoured equal pay for equal work — strong support for economic equality, suggesting the youth sampled are fairly gender sensitized on this dimension.

Do you favour systems that guarantee gender diversity in the government/educational institutions/executive positions? (98 responses)

70.4% favoured systems guaranteeing gender diversity and representation; 13.3% did not, and 16.3% were unsure.

Do you think gender-based discrimination happens in your home, workplace or community? (98 responses)

61.2% believed gender-based discrimination takes place around them; 24.5% reported none, and 14.3% were unsure or unaware.

Do you favour the use of gender-neutral language everywhere to promote equality? (99 responses)

69.7% favoured gender-neutral language to promote equality; 9.1% considered it unnecessary, and 21.2% were somewhat supportive but unsure.

Should girls be encouraged to pursue interests and careers traditionally associated with men? (99 responses)

84.8% encouraged girls to pursue traditionally male-associated careers; 10.1% were somewhat supportive but unsure, and 5.1% were against it.

Do you believe that a man should have the final say about decisions in his home? (99 responses)

66.7% disagreed that a man should have the final say in household decisions; 18.2% agreed, and 15.2% were unsure — meaning roughly one-third of respondents still lean toward patriarchal decision-making norms.

4.6 Gender-based Violence

Do you think a woman should tolerate violence of any kind to keep her family together? (98 responses)

70.4% disagreed that a woman should tolerate violence to keep her family together; 24.5% agreed, and 5.1% were unsure. Violence in any form contradicts the concept of equality — though nearly a quarter of respondents validating it on certain grounds points to a persisting gap.

Do you think making the opposite gender uncomfortable by cracking sexist jokes is okay? (99 responses)

76.8% were against making the opposite gender uncomfortable through sexist jokes; 17.2% considered it fine, and 6% were unsure but somewhat supportive.

Do you think gender sensitisation programs can help reduce incidents of gender-based violence? (98 responses)

63.3% believed gender sensitization programs can help reduce gender-based violence; 14.3% considered it unnecessary, and 22.4% were unsure but somewhat supportive — reflecting a broadly progressive and aware respondent base.

Chapter 5Conclusion and Recommendations

The following conclusions can be drawn from the data above:

  • Nearly 60% of the young population is not aware of the difference between sex and gender, often using the two words interchangeably. Sex refers to biological and physiological characteristics, while gender refers to the socially constructed norms, roles, and relationships of men and women.
  • Nearly 60% of the population has never received training or education on gender-related issues.
  • On average, around 65% of the young population does not believe in pre-defined gender stereotypes.
  • On average, around 78% does not believe in pre-set gender roles — though there is a possibility of a gap between stated beliefs and actual behaviour.
  • On average, around 74% favours gender equality in all spheres of life.
  • On average, around 70% is against gender-based violence for any cause.
On average, around 70% of the young population of Kanpur city is fairly gender-sensitized.

However, there is still more that needs to be done to sensitize the young population and ultimately achieve gender equality. There is a clear lack of training among youth with regard to sensitization — likely due to a lack of gender sensitization programs in existence, or negligence in their implementation.

Furthermore, an average of 78% of the young population actively supports including gender sensitization workshops, training, or curriculum as part of school and workplace activity. Only a few schools in India — such as Kendriya Vidyalaya — currently educate students through curricula in this domain. This should have wider reach, since gender sensitization can only be inculcated through education. At the administrative level, policy implementation should be carried out properly, with feedback loops in place to continuously improve these policies.
